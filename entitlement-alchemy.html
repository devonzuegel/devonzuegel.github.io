Land use alchemy: Why cheap land is cheap & how to turn it into gold

March 2026

The following is a writeup of my notes on where the value comes from in entitlement-driven development. My original goal was to build a simple model I can use to stress-test deals, and I figured others might find it interesting too.

Friends often send me links to beautiful pieces of land with prices that seem too good to be true. I'm honored that people think of me, but these conversations almost always go the same way. When we look into the zoning, it quickly explains the price: you're not allowed to build much on it. The land isn't "entitled."

"Entitlement" is the legal process by which raw land gets permission to become housing. In California, that process is long, expensive, uncertain, and controlled by local governments. Cheap land is cheap because the market has correctly priced in that getting permission to build on it is somewhere between very hard and nearly impossible. The discount isn't a bargain — it's a reflection of how much work it takes to unlock the value.

However, if you do succeed in getting land entitled for a significant amount of development, the land's value jumps to reflect the new permitted supply.

I call this "entitlement alchemy" because you're essentially conjuring value from thin air — the land doesn't change, the dirt doesn't move, but the piece of paper that says "you may build here" can double the value overnight. In supply-constrained markets, the permit is a huge portion of the product's value. Everything else — roads, utilities, infrastructure, the homes themselves — is execution.

1. N — entitled units

Before entitlement, land is priced assuming no new supply will emerge from it. When you successfully entitle, you've created units the market didn't expect to exist. This is where the return comes from. Without N > 0, nothing else matters.

2. The profit function

A key feature of infrastructure cost is that much of it is fixed regardless of unit count — the roads, utilities, and grading don't scale linearly with every additional lot. This means more units not only increases total profit but also increases profit per unit, since total_infra_cost gets spread across more lots.

profit(N) = N * price_per_lot - total_infra_cost - land_cost

The slope is set by price_per_lot — small changes here rotate the line a lot. The intercept is shifted down by total_infra_cost and land_cost, both of which are largely fixed, which is why squeezing more units out of an entitlement is so valuable.

3. carry_cost — time × rate

The third variable is cost of capital. It compounds with time because your investors or lenders are charging you interest the entire time the project is running, which means a one-year delay is economically equivalent to a meaningful increase in the effective cost of your infrastructure.

carry_cost = total_infra_cost * interest_rate * years profit(N) = N * price_per_lot - total_infra_cost - total_infra_cost * interest_rate * years // carry - land_cost

This is obviously a simplified model — it ignores absorption pace, financing structure, product mix, and a lot else. But for first-order intuition about where value is created and which variables to stress-test, it's been useful to me.
