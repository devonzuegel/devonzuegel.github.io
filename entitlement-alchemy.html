Land use alchemy: Why cheap land is cheap & how to turn it into gold

April 2026

Here are my notes about where land value comes from in entitlement-driven development. My original goal was to build a simple model I can use to stress-test deals, and I figured others might find it interesting too.

Friends often send me links to beautiful pieces of land with prices that are too good to be true. I'm honored that people think of me when they see great land, but these conversations almost always go the same way. When we look into the zoning, it quickly explains the price: you're not allowed to build much on it. The land isn't "entitled."

"Entitlement" is the legal process by which raw land gets permission to be developed. In California, that process is long, expensive, uncertain, and controlled by local governments. Cheap land is cheap because the market has correctly priced in that getting permission to build on it is very hard, or in some cases nearly impossible. The discount usually isn't a bargain — it's a reflection of how much work it takes to unlock the value.

However, if you do succeed in getting land entitled for a significant amount of development, the land's value shoots up to reflect the new permitted supply.

I call this "entitlement alchemy" because you're essentially conjuring value from thin air — the dirt doesn't move, but the piece of paper that says "you may build here" can double the value overnight. In supply-constrained markets, the permission to build is a huge portion of the asset's value.

Fixed Infrastructure Cost, Variable Unit Count

A key feature of infrastructure cost is that much of it is fixed regardless of unit count — the roads, utilities, and grading don't scale linearly with every additional lot. This means more units not only increases total profit but also increases profit per unit, since total_infra_cost gets spread across more lots.

profit(N) = N * price_per_lot - total_infra_cost - land_cost

The slope is set by price_per_lot — small changes here rotate the line a lot. The intercept is shifted down by total_infra_cost and land_cost, both of which are largely fixed, which is why squeezing more units out of an entitlement is so valuable.

The intercept between the black line and the x-axis represents the point where profit equals zero, indicating the minimum number of units that must be sold to break even on the infrastructure investment.

The effective cost of infrastructure compounds over time (if you use debt)

The third variable is cost of capital. It compounds with time because your investors or lenders are charging you interest the entire time the project is running, which means a one-year delay is economically equivalent to a meaningful increase in the effective cost of your infrastructure.

carry_cost = total_infra_cost * interest_rate * years profit(N) = N * price_per_lot - total_infra_cost - total_infra_cost * interest_rate * years // carry - land_cost

This is obviously a simplified model — it ignores absorption pace, financing structure, product mix, and a lot else. But for first-order intuition about where value is created and which variables to stress-test, it's been useful to me.
